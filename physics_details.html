Physics

Core Science Field Details

Field Summary

Physics is the fundamental natural science concerned with understanding the universe at its most basic level. It studies the properties of matter, energy, space, and time, and the interactions between them. Modern physics is broadly divided between classical concepts (mechanics, thermodynamics) and modern concepts (relativity, quantum mechanics), which collectively explain phenomena from the smallest subatomic particles to the largest galaxies. Its pursuit aims to discover the universal laws that govern all natural processes.

Key Sub-Fields

Theoretical Physics

Focuses on developing abstract mathematical models and conceptual frameworks to understand and explain physical systems. This branch guides experimental physics by predicting results, and it often ventures into areas like string theory or grand unified theories, aiming for a consistent description of nature.

Mathematical Physics

The use of mathematics to solve problems in physics, often concerning the mathematical structure and rigor of physical theories. It ensures that physics principles are mathematically sound, often involving advanced areas like differential geometry, functional analysis, and group theory.

Classical Mechanics

Also known as Newtonian Mechanics. It describes the motion of macroscopic objects under the action of forces. This branch is highly accurate for non-relativistic velocities and remains crucial for engineering, orbital mechanics, and everyday physical systems.

Thermodynamics

Governs the relationships between heat, work, temperature, and energy at a macroscopic level. Its three laws dictate the limits of energy conversion efficiency and explain phase transitions (solid, liquid, gas), making it vital for power generation and chemical processes.

Quantum Mechanics

The revolutionary framework describing physical reality at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles. It introduced concepts like wave-particle duality and quantization, forming the basis for modern electronics, lasers, nuclear energy, and our understanding of chemical bonding.

Electromagnetism

The study of the electromagnetic force, which arises from electric charges and fields. It is unified by Maxwell's equations, explaining phenomena like electricity, magnetism, and light as aspects of the same force. It underpins all modern communications and electrical engineering.

Nuclear Physics

Investigates the structure of the atomic nucleus, the fundamental particles within it (protons and neutrons), and their interactions. Key areas include nuclear stability, fission (energy generation), fusion (star power), and the applications of radioactivity in medicine and archaeology.

Solid State Physics

Examines the physical properties of rigid materials (solids), particularly those with crystal structures, such as semiconductors, metals, and insulators. It studies how quantum mechanics and electromagnetic properties give rise to electrical conductivity, magnetism, and superconductivity, driving modern computing and materials science.

High-Energy Physics

(Often called Particle Physics). This branch seeks to understand the most basic fundamental particles (e.g., quarks, leptons) and the forces between them. Experiments often involve massive particle accelerators to probe the conditions of the early universe.

Acoustics

The science of sound, including its generation, transmission, reception, and effects. It encompasses the study of mechanical waves in various media and has applications ranging from music and architectural design to medical ultrasound and underwater sonar.

Optics

The branch of physics that studies the behavior and properties of light (visible, infrared, and ultraviolet radiation). It covers reflection, refraction, diffraction, and interference, forming the foundation for lenses, telescopes, fiber optics, and photonics technology.
